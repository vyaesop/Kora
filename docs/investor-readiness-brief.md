# Kora Investor Readiness Brief

Date: April 25, 2026  
Status: Founder-ready working draft for investor conversations, diligence prep, and strategic partner meetings

## 1. Executive Summary

Kora is a mobile-first freight marketplace and shipment operations platform built for Ethiopia. It helps cargo owners discover transport capacity, helps drivers find loads that fit their routes and equipment, and gives both sides one shared workflow for bidding, acceptance, communication, live delivery updates, settlement, and post-delivery closeout.

The company thesis is straightforward:

- Freight coordination in Ethiopia is still fragmented across calls, brokers, messaging apps, and cash-based trust.
- That fragmentation creates slower matching, lower utilization, more empty return trips, and weaker transparency for both cargo owners and drivers.
- A localized logistics platform can create value not just at listing time, but across the full shipment lifecycle: discovery, trust, execution, and payment.

Kora is not only a load board. It is positioned to become a lightweight operating system for domestic freight coordination in a market where low-bandwidth use, local payment rails, and trust-building product flows matter more than polished but imported software assumptions.

## 2. What Kora Does

Kora connects two primary user groups:

- Cargo owners who need freight moved quickly and safely.
- Drivers who want consistent, profitable loads and better route utilization.

Today, the product covers the core journey from load posting to delivery completion:

- Load posting with route, packaging, weight, and cargo classification.
- Marketplace feed for open loads.
- Driver bidding and cargo-owner bid review.
- Verification and trust controls for marketplace participation.
- Shipment status updates and basic delivery workflow management.
- In-app notifications and event visibility.
- Wallet and payment infrastructure for escrow-like settlement behavior.
- Route-aware suggestions that improve driver utilization.

## 3. Problem Statement

In many Ethiopian freight workflows, counterparties still coordinate through disconnected channels and informal trust networks. That creates operational pain on both sides.

For cargo owners:

- Slow driver discovery.
- Limited price transparency.
- Weak tracking after a load is assigned.
- Manual follow-up across multiple channels.
- Higher risk when working with unknown counterparties.

For drivers:

- Unpredictable demand.
- Deadhead return trips that destroy route economics.
- Limited visibility into which loads are worth bidding on.
- Delayed or unclear payment expectations.
- Weak digital proof of work history and reliability.

These are not just convenience problems. They are unit-economics, trust, and utilization problems.

## 4. Why Now

The timing case for Kora rests on four market conditions:

1. Smartphone-led coordination is already replacing purely offline dispatch behavior, even when workflows remain messy and fragmented.
2. Users increasingly expect app-level visibility, not just phone-call coordination.
3. Domestic digital payment rails are improving, creating room for embedded settlement.
4. Logistics participants in Ethiopia still lack a purpose-built system optimized for local operating realities, especially outside dense urban corridors.

This creates an opening for a vertical product that is not a generic marketplace clone, but a freight tool designed around Ethiopian routes, payment habits, verification needs, and bandwidth constraints.

## 5. Product Overview

Kora should be understood as a platform with five connected layers.

### 5.1 Marketplace Layer

- Cargo owners publish loads.
- Drivers browse, filter, and bid.
- The marketplace feed becomes the central discovery surface.

### 5.2 Trust Layer

- Identity and document verification.
- Profile visibility and status controls.
- Structured bid and delivery history over time.

### 5.3 Execution Layer

- Shared shipment detail thread.
- Bid acceptance and shipment lifecycle updates.
- Driver tracking and delivery-state progression.
- Communication and issue-reporting flows.

### 5.4 Financial Layer

- Wallet balances.
- Reserved funds before bid acceptance.
- Settlement release on delivery completion.
- Telebirr-aligned payment architecture for local monetization and checkout.

### 5.5 Intelligence Layer

- Route-aware suggestions.
- Driver return-load discovery.
- Search and filtering by route and cargo class.
- Future potential for pricing intelligence, lane density, and demand forecasting.

## 6. Core Product Features

This section is intentionally broader than the most recent implementation cycle. It reflects the product as a whole.

### 6.1 Cargo Owner Features

- Post and manage loads.
- Define origin, destination, packaging, and weight.
- Select broad cargo categories and specific subtypes.
- Review incoming bids and accept the best-fit driver.
- Track assigned shipments and view live progress.
- Message or contact counterparties from the shipment workflow.
- Review wallet balance before accepting transport commitments.
- Access notifications for bid events, shipment milestones, and account actions.

### 6.2 Driver Features

- Browse live feed of available loads.
- Filter by route and cargo classification.
- Submit and update bids.
- Track accepted work in one place.
- Receive suggestions for next loads based on current route/location context.
- Surface nearby follow-on opportunities to reduce empty return trips.
- Access notifications for bid responses, shipment updates, and account actions.
- Build a verified operating profile over time.

### 6.3 Shared Operational Features

- Shipment detail thread as the single source of truth.
- Delivery status progression.
- In-app chat and direct contact shortcuts.
- Issue and dispute reporting.
- Basic proof-of-delivery workflow.
- Ratings and post-delivery trust signals.

## 7. Product Principles

Kora should continue to be built around these principles:

- Low-friction first use: the product must be usable by operators who are not highly technical.
- Low-bandwidth resilience: network requests should be minimized, cached, and tolerant of weaker connectivity.
- Clarity over novelty: users need obvious next steps, not hidden workflows.
- Route economics over vanity engagement: the product should optimize earnings, utilization, and completion speed.
- Local fit over imported pattern-matching: payment, language, geography, and logistics behavior must reflect Ethiopia.

## 8. Market Opportunity

Kora’s immediate opportunity is domestic road freight coordination in Ethiopia, with a natural expansion path into adjacent logistics tooling.

The relevant market is not just “load boards.” It includes:

- Informal broker-mediated freight matching.
- Manual dispatch coordination.
- Cross-channel shipment communication and follow-up.
- Cash or semi-manual settlement flows.
- Lightweight fleet and route coordination for independent drivers and SMEs.

The practical initial wedge is clear:

- Start with owner-driver and SME cargo-owner matching.
- Win on discovery, trust, and route utilization.
- Expand into deeper workflow ownership and monetization once transaction volume grows.

## 9. Customer Segments

Primary segments:

- SME cargo owners with recurring regional shipments.
- Independent drivers and small fleets.
- Traders moving agricultural, retail, livestock, construction, and liquid goods.

Secondary segments:

- Brokers who may use the platform as an execution layer.
- Mid-market shippers needing more visibility without enterprise software complexity.
- Strategic logistics partners, financiers, or insurers that benefit from verified shipment activity.

## 10. Competitive Positioning

Kora’s defensibility is unlikely to come from “having an app.” It comes from local operational fit and workflow depth.

Potential differentiators:

- Built specifically for Ethiopian freight behavior and geography.
- Better support for route continuity and return-load logic.
- Verification and trust controls embedded into the transaction path.
- Payment architecture designed around local rails instead of card-first assumptions.
- Product direction that respects low-connectivity constraints.

## 11. Business Model

Kora can monetize through multiple layers, in sequence rather than all at once.

### 11.1 Near-Term Monetization

- Take rate or service fee on successful transactions.
- Bid acceptance or settlement fee.
- Wallet-based transaction revenue.

### 11.2 Mid-Term Monetization

- Premium driver visibility or ranking placement.
- Priority load placement for cargo owners.
- Fleet or business subscriptions for operational tools.
- Value-added verification or trust products.

### 11.3 Longer-Term Monetization

- Embedded financial services.
- Insurance or guarantee partnerships.
- Data products around lane demand, pricing, and reliability.

## 12. Go-To-Market Strategy

The GTM strategy should be operationally focused rather than brand-first.

### 12.1 Supply Acquisition

- Recruit drivers in specific high-frequency corridors first.
- Use route-density messaging: more jobs, fewer empty returns, faster next-load discovery.
- Build trust with verification and visible work history.

### 12.2 Demand Acquisition

- Target cargo owners with recurring regional shipping needs.
- Position Kora as a faster and more transparent alternative to phone-only coordination.
- Show clear time-to-bid and time-to-assignment benefits.

### 12.3 Corridor Strategy

- Focus on a limited set of high-activity lanes first.
- Win density on those routes before broad geographic expansion.
- Use route liquidity as the core marketplace flywheel.

## 15. Technology and Platform Readiness

The current stack supports the core marketplace direction:

- Flutter client for cross-platform product delivery.
- Node/Express backend with Prisma-managed data model.
- Realtime event support and in-app notifications.
- Wallet and payment-order architecture.
- Driver location and map-based workflow support.

Near-term technical priorities should include:

- Payment completion and reconciliation hardening.
- Admin and support tooling for operations teams.

## 16. Risk Assessment

### 16.1 Marketplace Liquidity Risk

If either side of the marketplace remains too thin in a lane, matching quality suffers.

Mitigation:

- Corridor-first rollout.
- Supply-demand balancing by lane.
- Focus on repeat users and route density before broad expansion.

### 16.2 Trust and Fraud Risk

Unknown counterparties increase hesitation and failure risk.

Mitigation:

- Verification, ratings, delivery history, and structured communication.

### 16.3 Payment Risk

Embedded payments introduce operational and compliance complexity.

Mitigation:

- Start with controlled wallet mechanics and staged payment rollout.
- Expand payout automation only with confirmed provider coverage and reconciliation controls.

### 16.4 Connectivity Risk

Low-quality internet can degrade product reliability.

Mitigation:

- Strong client-side caching.
- Stale fallback reads.
- Minimized dependency on constant refetching.

### 16.5 Operational Support Risk

Disputes, failed deliveries, and onboarding friction can create support burden.

Mitigation:

- Clear workflow states.
- Better admin review surfaces.
- Better instrumentation around failure points.
